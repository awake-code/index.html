    Chicago Specific Types of Crime Counts (2016)

    Crime in Chicago during 2016 is heavily concentrated within a small number of offense categories. Theft alone, not even counting motor vehicle theft, robbery or burglary, accounts for a significantly larger share of reported incidents than any other category. Meanwhile, the majority of crime types occur at very low percentages or do not appear at all in the sample, showing an uneven distribution in crime types.

    Chicago Specific Types of Crime Counts (2024)

    The same types of crime dominate in 2024 as did in 2016 in Chicago. The consistent and disproportionate occurence of theft and battery indicates that economic problems during COVID didn't alter the most common crimes. Rather, these offenses are a result of long-standing problems. However, decreases in robbery and burglary indicate some progress.

    Chicago Specific Types of Crime Counts (2016 vs 2024)

    The visualization shows that the most common crime types in Chicago remain relatively consistent across the years. However, there are more noticeable differences among mid-frequency crimes, suggesting changes in specific crime types. Additionally, no single year stands out as "dominant," or having consistently higher crime levels across all categories, indicating crime trends fluctuate by type rather than moving as one trend.

    Chicago Types of Crime Counts by Percentage (2016)

    The pie chart, created from the Chicago 2016 data set, shows that theft makes up approximately one quarter of all reported crimes, making it the single largest category. However, a substantial portion of crimes is accounted for by multiple crime types with frequencies that individually are about 5-7%. This is important to note, because when added together these "seemingly small percentages" collectively exceed the frequency of theft. Additionally, through the visualization of the pie chart, criminal damages emerges as a significant share of Chicago crimes, which wasn't as apparent in the bar plot.

    Chicago Types of Crime Counts by Percentage (2024)

    The 2024 data shows no emergence of new major crime types, suggesting there is no single, definitive post-COVID shift in the overall structure of crime. However, there is a notable increase in motor vehicle theft, which may reflect the financial stress following the 2020 economic crisis and the need to obtain money quickly. Additionally, the rise in assaults could be associated with lingering mental health problems and substance abuse issues, triggered by the pandemic period.
